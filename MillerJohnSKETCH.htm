John Earnest Miller

(1918 - 2012)

Johnny

 

 

This sketch was written by his daughter-in-law, Suzanne (Purdue) Miller, and read at his funeral.  John was a truly amazing, giving man -- such a blessing to our family. -- bk

 

John Earnest Miller

       John Earnest Miller was born on May 23, 1918, in his parent’s home. John, his older sister, Mary, his parents, Emma and Percy, all lived on East Sparta Ave. in Howenstine. Percy worked within walking distance of home at the tile/brick plant as a engineer and machinist. John’s father, Percy, died in December of 1918 during the great flu epidemic leaving Emma to rear Mary, almost 4, and John, six months.  She provided for them by doing laundry, mending, and taking in boarders. She married one of her boarders, Frank Miller, and they had John’s younger brother, Robert.

       When John was seven, he lost his right eye while trying to fix a barbed wire fence. He was twisting the taut wire on a stick, when the stick broke sending the wire flying. John never let his limited vision slow him down. In fact this may have given him some of the tenacity he exhibited his whole life.

       John attended Howenstine School, a one room school house, for eight years. In 1935, the school was moved and turned in to a church. John helped with the move and transformation. While attending elementary school, John helped his mother with chores at home. This included helping with Emma’s beloved flower and vegetable garden. John told us at this time of his life, he just didn’t like gardening. What a wonderful gardener he turned out to be. Last summer, John still tended his beloved strawberry patch putting out runners for this spring’s crop of berries.  Just last week, he talked about the spring flowers, strawberries, and tomatoes.

       John attended Canton Township High School for four years. While in school, John was a great student receiving high marks. He always loved math, especially algebra, but said that he could see no point learning the names of bugs in biology. He played baseball and was the student manager for both the football and softball teams. John graduated in 1936 with honors.

       After high school, John got a job at the A and P store working in Canton and then the Tile Co. in East Sparta. He continued to play ball. Then in 1940, he began working for the Hercules Engine Company in Canton. John always did piece work because a fast worker could make more money. With John’s work ethic, he certainly did make more money. John worked for Hercules for forty-two years and always doing piece work. He used to say that some younger coworkers would comment that they just couldn’t keep up with him. We all know that when John was motivated, it got done!

       In the late spring of 1941, John met Elsie Clauser. He would often eat at the QP Diner across the street from the Hercules plant. That’s where he spotted the new waitress, a brown-haired beauty, who had just graduated from Minerva High School.  John and Elsie dated for about six months. John’s mother, Emma, told John, “The only reason Elsie is dating you is because you have a car.” That wasn’t the truth. Elsie and John truly loved each other celebrating seventy years of marriage on November 20, 2011.

       Then in 1942 John joined the Army. He was first stationed for a year in England. His vision keep him from combat duty. John became a sergeant working as both a purchasing agent for the mess hall and being in charge of the barracks. In 1943 he returned to Ohio for duty at Crile Hospital rejoining his wife and young son, also named John. John and Elsie spent the remaining years of the war living and working in Cleveland.

       In 1945 John, Elsie, and little Johnny returned to Canton. They moved into a small house on old route 8 right next door to John’s mother, Emma. Their second son, Bobby, was born that year. John returned to work at Hercules Engine Company. He was able to resume playing ball with Stark Ceramics. John was the pitcher specializing in the curve ball. He led his team to the state championship one year.

       In 1949 John and Elsie moved into their new house on Cleveland Ave. South. It was only a few blocks from their old house and grandma’s. John lived the rest of his life in this house. In 1949 Elsie and John had their daughter, Bonnie, to be a blessing and round out their family.

       After returning from the Army, John continued to be involved with Canton South sports. By 1946, he was a charter member of the Canton South Booster Club. Coach Red Ash wrote this about John, “Not only have you given generously of your time and efforts for the little leaguers in our area, but your loyalty to Canton South athletics is unsurpassed. From the time you raised money on your own to buy the first camera for the athletic program in 1946 until the present time, no one in the community has done more than you have for our Booster Club.”

        John attended football, basketball, baseball, and softball games at South through 2011. Just this season, John gave up his top row season ticket seat at the basketball games for a lower seat. Just this last Saturday, while in the hospital, John asked if South won their Friday night basketball game.

       In 1955 John recognized the need for a little league field in Howenstine. Up to this time, the boys had to play in North Industry. A local field would give more boys a chance to play. John built with the help of a neighbor, Lee Rodriguez, and Homer Jackson the field on Cleveland Ave. South. It truly was John’s “field of dreams” that countless boys have enjoyed. Bobby got to play one year of little league there with his dad as manager. After Bob was too old for little league, John began years as a coach.

                Even though John did not play official games on the field, he did get to see Bob enjoy the field playing softball for Howenstine Christian Church.

       The ball field has been such a big part of John’s life for so many years. It truly was his baby. He cared for it and kept it immaculate. There were no stones or weeds on his infield. This past fall, John could be seen on the field with his pocket knife picking out small blades of grass. Whenever John disappeared, Elsie usually found him at his beloved ball field. John spent so much time there that Elsie even had a special name for the field!

       John has been nominated for many awards in his lifetime. Two of his favorites were being inducted to both the Old Timers Fast Pitch Softball Hall of Fame and the Greater Stark County Amateur Baseball Hall of Fame.

       As the retirement years passed John kept busy helping others. He delivered meals on wheels, worked on school levies, took care of the church, shoveled snow for many neighbors, grew and shared many, many quarts of strawberries, corn, tomatoes, and other vegetables, and doing countless other acts of kindness.

        John was a man of integrity, intelligent, selfless, and hard working---blessing all those who knew him. We are lucky to be able to call him-- husband, father, grandfather, great-grandfather, friend, coach, and  a man of God.  We need not doubt that he is now with is Savior keeping those ball fields in Paradise in shape.
