1860

 

Alexander, Wm. Alexander

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 8 February 1860

     DIED—December 14th, 1859, in Cedar County, Iowa, formerly of this County, WM. ALEXANDER, only son of Westwood and Amanda Jane Wilson, aged 8 years and 8 days.

     “And hast thou left me love!

        My fair sweet rose!

     Thou’st joined the blest above

        Beyond earth’s throes.

     And shall thy smile no more

        Its soothing sweetness being

     Like Eden sunshine o’er

        My sorrowing!

.

     I have no place of rest

       Far thou art fled,

     The ice is on my breast

       My love is dead!

     The cords about my heart

       Are shaken—thrilled, and sere—

     ‘Tis bitterness to part

         With one so dear.

.

     Farewell! As thou dost wander

       Beyond my sight,

     No love than ours was fonder

       My heart’s delight!

     But we shall meet above,

       To part no more,

     Where blooms my angel love

       On that blest shore.”

BY A FRIEND

 

Atkinson, George B. Atkinson

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 11 January 1860

     GEORGE B. ATKINSON, departed this life December 27th, 1859, at his residence in Boston, Belmont County, Ohio, aged 57 years and 8 months; after an illness of near six months, which arose from Chronic Bronchitis, but ultimately resulting in paralysis.

     He appeared conscious that his end was nigh, and seemingly awaited God’s pleasure, in removing him home.  He was an honorable member of the M. E. Church for 19 years, in which he gave full evidence of living the life of a devoted Christian.  He leaves a pleasant home, brightened by the smiles of his affectionate children, and endeared by the presence of a fond companion who is left; together with a large circle of friends and relatives to morn his loss; but they have a happy consolation:  that their loss, is his great gain, for the scriptures say, “Blessed are the dead that die in the Lord:” It is God that has bereft us:  He can all our sorrows heal.”

     “Mourn not ye loved ones left behind,

        But wait by faith the Master’s time;

     His grace sustains the trembling mind,

       While mercy whispers, I am thine.

     WM. S. WINNETT.

 

Barker, Hon. Joseph Barker

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 25 January 1860

The Marietta papers notice the death of Hon. Joseph Barker, the oldest white native of Ohio.  He was born at the mouth of the Muskingum in 1790, and was the fourth white male born in the North Western Territory.

 

Booth, Camilla Booth

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 1 February 1860

     DIED—In Clarington, Jan. 19th, of Inflammation of the brain, CAMILLA, youngest child of W. F. and E. Booth, aged 4 years, 10 months and 15 days.

     Camilla was a tender bud of much promise, and early displayed a strong attachment to things of a Divine nature.  To one of her moral qualities the clime of Heaven was better suited than that of earth.

     The early departure of one so lovely has excited feeling of deep sorrow which nothing can sooth but the soul cheering truth that “of such is the kingdom of Heaven.”

         And lay them in my breast;

J. F. J.

 

Brailsford, Barney Brailsford

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 18 January 1860

    The man found dead near Suppers’ school house, as noticed by us last week, was an elderly man named Barney Brailsford.  He had started to mill with his grain on his back, in company with his son.  He sat down on a stump to rest himself, and his son went on.  In a short time afterward he was found by some school children, lying by the side of the stump, dead.  No marks of violence were found upon his person, and it is probable that his death was caused by habitual intoxication.

Carman, S. O. Carman

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 13 June 1860

     Dead Body Found.

     A dead body was found floating in the river two miles below Clarington, on the 2nd inst.  An inquest was held by E. B. Cox, Esquire, and the verdict of the jury was, that the deceased came to his death by drowning.  He had on a dark cloth or cassimere frock coat; his pant were of a dark green color, and considerably worn; his hair was dark brown or nearly black; in person he was rather short and heavy set.  The following articles were found upon his person:  a pocket knife with four blades and a pearl handle, a pair of spectacles with brass frames, two small keys, a pocket comb, a lead pencil, a memorandum book, a porte-monale containing several notes of hand and other papers mostly dated at Union, Ohio.  From the papers it appears that his name was S. O. Carman.  The memorandum book contained a journal of his travels this spring, showing that he has been at Chicago, Zanesville, Wheeling, Philadelphia, Bordentown and other places.

 

Clark, Mrs. Jane Clark

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 25 April 1860

     For the Spirit

     On Sunday the 1st inst. after an illness of three days, MRS. JANE CLARK, wife of Joseph C. Clark, of Summerfield O. in the 23rd year of her age.

     Not dead, but gone home to God!

     How delightful to know that death is not the end of life; but only a change of its form and manner.  How blessed the assurance that our young friend, whose loss we now mourn, is not lost, but only gone before us to enjoy that higher life, whose loves and affections the rude hand of death can never mar.

     I have enjoyed and intimate acquaintance with Mr. And Mrs. Clark since their happy marriage, and never have I known a more loving, confiding wife, or a more humble devoted Christian than she was.

      Her last illness was very severe, but she bore her sufferings with fortitude; and on Sunday morning at eleven o’clock, she fell asleep, with a smile of Heavenly quiet lighting up her features.

.

“Like morning light too soon withdrawn,

       So didst thou pass away;

     Thy gentle spirit from us gone,

       Has closed an opening day.

.

     As sinks the babe in slumbers sweet,

       So hast thou gone to rest,

     And gently faded from our sight,

       To live among the blest.

.

     The flower just bloomed, but not to fade,

       To other hands is given;

     The outer form in dust is laid,

       The inner blooms in Heaven!

.

     Ah not in vain our tears are shed;

       They are the gifts of of (sic) love

     To one, here numbered with the dead,

       But gone to live above.

.

     No more we’ll weep; but strew fair flowers

       Around thy earthly tomb.

     Thy home is now in heavenly bowers,

       Where brighter flowers do bloom.”

SUMMERFELD, O.                     W. W.

Duvall, Mary Duvall

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 15 February 1860

       DIED—On the 7th day of February, in Green township, at the residence of her son, MARY DUVALL, in the 95th year of her age.

       Mrs. Duvall has resided in this county about 36 years, previous to which she lived in Green county, Pennsylvania.  She has been a widow for over 28 years.

       During her childhood she attended school in the neighborhood.  One morning her stepfather said to her mother, “I think we will not let Polly go to school today: I have had strange feeling about the Indians.”  Her mother tried to laugh away what seemed his superstitious fears, but he persisted, and said, “it will be but one day lost from school anyhow and we will not let her go.”  She was consequently detained at home, and in about three or four hours, they heard the news of the school teacher and all the scholars being killed by the savages.  She met with many other adventures, and made other escapes from the Indians during her early life.

She had been, at the time of her death a consistent and faithful member of the Christian Church for more than 30 years.  She lived a life which led all her neighbors to say that she was as near perfection as it was possible for humanity to be.  Rarely indeed is an example set so worthy of imitation.

     She had many descendants to the fifth generation; and could say “rise up daughter and go to thy daughter, for thy daughter’s daughter has a daughter.”

     Few persons live to so great an age and fewer still retain their faculties in as great perfection as she did.

Evans, Mary A. Evans

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 8 February 1860

[From the Greene Co. Pa, Republican]

     --On the 24 inst., at her residence in this place, Mrs. MARY A. EVANS, wife of Lewis K. Evans, in the 22d year of her age.

     The deceased was a daughter of Mr. Wm. Steel, of Monroe county, Ohio, and a member of the Wesleyan Methodist Church, of that place, having joined when only twelve years old.  Although her acquaintance here was short, sort as it was, she endeared herself to all that knew her, by  her gentleness of manner and kindness of heart.  Bereft of her only child, who died but a few days before, and suffering under affliction and pain; yet, in view of death, she was calm and serene.  How beautiful and expressive her language:--“Death! Call it not death—it is only a sweet sleep.  Victory! Victory! My only trouble is that my husband and mother grieve for me.  Grieve not for me, but meet me in Heaven.”  Thus she spake as husband and mother bent over her, watching every word and anticipating every wish.  “Did you hear that sweet music from angels harps. Oh! I thought I was almost within the heavenly gates.”—Christ is extending to me the golden harp, and beckons me to come.”  “I love you Lewis, but I must love Christ first.”

     She requested singing and prayer, and sang sweetly the “Christian’s Hope” without assistance.  Although she had many endearing relations to bind her to earth, yet she yielded all without a murmur, and pillowed her head on ‘Jesus’ breast, and breathed her life out sweetly there.

     ‘Tis religion that can give

     Sweetest pleasures while we live;

     ‘Tis religion can supply

     Solid comforts when we die.

     After death its joys will be

     Lasting as Eternity

     DIED—On the 8th day of January, 1860, Mrs. HANNAH MARIAH LINDAMOOD, widow of Edmond Lindamood, of Bethel township, Monroe county, Ohio, in the 27th year of her age.

      Another worth Disciple of our blessed Saviour has gone to her reward.  For several years she was known as a consistent member of the Lutheran Church, but as an exemplary Christian in heart and life.  During her last illness, which continued only 9 days, she bore her affliction with a fortitude that only Divine Grace could have enabled her to manifest, and with a resignation that banished all anxiety from her heart, as she felt that she was ready to depart, and be with Christ which is far better.  Her remains were consigned in the narrow house of clay, in the graveyard.  She left a husband and 4 children to mourn her loss.

McCollough, Margery McCollough

Source:  Spirit of Democracy December 28, 1859

       On Friday evening, Dec. 9th, in Beallsville, Monroe County, Ohio, of Acute Rheumatism, after a short illness, Mrs. MARGERY, wife of Dr. J. G. McCollough, aged 26 years, 9 months and 19 days.

     The deceased was beloved by all who knew her.  Her hospitality and kindness will not soon be forgotten.  Whilst most affectionate and kind in all the relations of life, she was unassuming and retiring.

     Mrs. M. was reared up under Presbyterian influence.  And in the fall of 1858 she was on profession of her faith, received into this communion of the Presbyterian Church of Beallsville.  Prior to her connexion (sic) with the Church, and ever afterward, she gave full evidence of living a consistent and devoted christian.

     In this sudden and afflictive dispensation of Providence, this bereaved family have lost a member, in whom was blended all the sympathy, love and tenderness of a wife and mother.  A husband and two children, with numerous other relatives, mourn her absence; but they have the happy consolation “that their loss is her unspeakable gain.”  “She rests from her labors.”

      Mourn not ye loved ones left behind,

      But wait by faith the Master’s time;

      His grace sustains the trembling mind,

      While mercy whispers, “I am thine.”

     St. Clairsville and Cadiz papers please copy.

O’Connor, Hiram D O’Connor

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 6 June 1860

     DROWNED, at Clarington, on Saturday the 26th day of May, HIRAM D. only son of Dr. J. D. and Ruth O’Connor, aged eight years, three months and twenty-six days.

.

     When death comes slowly but surely to our dwellings, we are prepared to meet him; but when without one moments warning, a beloved child is thus suddenly and sadly taken, in life’s early morning, the stroke come to the hearts of the mourners with fearful force.

     Hiram is dead!  At home, there is a blank that never can be filled.  At school, a vacant seat—there, he is sadly missed.  He has gone.  But even in his death we think of him with pleasure; our remembrances of him are sweet.  Though he was very young, he had shown to those who knew him well, the promise of a useful man.  The little work he had to do, was well and promptly done.  He was never late at school.  He was an obedient, respectful, affectionate and truthful boy.  We knew not how strong a hold he had on our affections, till thus thorn away.  And if around a stranger’s heart he had entwined himself so closely, who shall attempt to measure the sorrow of fond parents over the grave of an only son?  We would not intrude upon your grief, dear friends, but ye cry out with one of old.  “Miserable comforters are ye all,” but rather weep with you, for “Jesus wept.”  But with your mourning may “Our Father” mingle hope.  You’ll meet your boy again.  He has a Father now, whose love for him is more tender than a mother’s.  Trust Him—for He can heal the broken hearted.  Trust Him—for “He doeth all things well.”

Randolph, James F. Randolph

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 11 January 1860

     Died, on Sunday morning, January 8th, 1860 of hemorrhage of the lungs, JAMES F. RANDOLPH, at the residence of his father, Joel F. Randolph, Esq., in Woodsfield.

      The deceased was born in Woodsfield, on the 18th day of August, 1835, and resided here nearly all his life.  Studious beyond most of his associates, and without the vices which sometimes lead youth astray, he early acquired a good education, and gave high promise of future usefulness.  After laborious preparation, he was admitted to the Bar at the August Term of the District Court in Monroe County, 1856.  No doubt he would have succeeded, for he lacked neither capacity, industry nor energy; but he had scarcely engaged in practice when he was appointed to a desk in the Treasury Department at Washington City.  There he discharged his duty in the utmost satisfactory manner, but his sedentary habits and close application to books impaired his health so much that during last summer he was compelled to return home.  Through the attention of his physicians and friends, however, he was so much improved as to return for a short time to Washington; but his disease had not been arrested; and he was soon compelled again to return home.  On Sunday morning no immediate danger was apprehended—indeed, hope was entertained that he would live many months if not entirely recover, but a slight fit of coughing brought on the hemorrhage, and suddenly the melancholy lesson was again forced upon us, the  “in the midst of life we are in death.”

     The writer of this brief sketch knew him well; he has full opportunity to know his honesty and truth and manly independence; and he feels how much kindness, how much sincere friendship, has gone out of the world; how much goodness really dwelt in that heart which has been so untimely stilled in death.  This slight tribute is all that he may now present at the grave—this and the offering which long friendship, warm and unbroken, ought not so withhold, and cannot.

     “Green be the turf above thee,

        Friend of my better days;

     None knew thee but to love thee,

       Nor named thee but to praise.”

     J. W. O.

Scott, Mary Anne Scott

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 7 March 1860     

     DIED—At the residence of her father, on the 26th ult., Mrs. MARY ANNE, consort of Mr. Adam Scott, of Washington Co., O., aged 22 years and 5 months.

     The subject of this notice, was a daughter of Mr. James Stallings, and was born in Belmont County, Ohio.  She embraced religion, or was born again, in Monroe county when about 12 years of age.  She “remembered her Creator in the days of her youth.” 

     She lived in the enjoyment of religion, and found it the one thing needful in life, and a divine reality in sickness and death.  She leaves behind her a bereaved husband, a little prattling girl, and other friends to mourn their loss, but not without hope.  She left them a rich legacy, a goodly example, which if they follow they will surely meet her in that world of bliss and glory, where sickness and sorrow, pain and death, are felt and feared no more.”

     Her disease was pulmonary consumption, under whose withering influence she lingered for many a year.  She bore her affliction with Christian fortitude and resignation, sustained by the grace of God.  As a daughter she was dutiful, as a wife affectionate, as a mother kind, as a Christian faithful.  Having lived the life of the righteous, she was permitted to die his death, and now she rests from her suffering.  May God comfort the bereaved ones.

     This occasion was improved by a discourse, by the writer, from Rev. 14—13.

     Marietta papers please copy.

Stafford Circuit, Pittsburg Conference.                            WM. GAMBLE, JR.,

Feb. 27, 1860

Workman, Amos Workman

Source:  Spirit of Democracy 13 June 1860

     At his residence in Adams township, this county, on the 27 day of April, 1860, AMOS WORKMAN, in the 36th year of his age.

     The deceased leaves a wife, 7 small children and a large circle of friends to mourn his departure, but, they mourn not as those that have no hope, for he gave abundant evidence of the power of the religion which he professed, to sustain the soul in a conflict with its last enemy.  His reason remained unimpaired to the last, and his dying conversation and experience are full of interest to the Christian.  As he approached the Jordan of Death, his prospects grew “  What a verification of the language of David.  “Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for thou art with me; thy rod and thy staff they comfort me.”  What a blessed thing thus to die calmly, peacefully, and full of hope.  The bereaved children are without the pious example, and the tender sympathies of a Christian father on earth.  Let them imitate his pious life and remember his dying advice that they may have comfort in their bereavement.  Then may they sing in hope and resignation:

     Dearest father thou hast left us,

       Here, thy loss we deeple (sic) feel;

     But’t is God who hath bereft us,

       He can all our sorrows heal.

.

     And again we hope to meet thee,

       When the day of life is fled,

     Then in Heaven with joy to greet three,

        Where no farewell tear is shed.”
