DATA USE POLICY

 

Genealogy has become a very popular field of research throughout much of the world.  It seems that most people have become increasingly interested in their family tree. The search goes beyond simply the names and dates of our ancestors.  Most researchers strive to learn more about their ancestors and their descendants as well as how we are related to them.  Few family lines do not have at least one member who is researching family genealogy. 

 

Genealogy can be a lot of work.  But the excitement and rewards of discovery usually offset the labor.  Most of the information that is gathered during genealogy research is public information.  It is available from newspapers, birth records, death records, marriage records, court records, passenger lists, citizenship records, and much more.  It is a matter of searching out the sources and recording, organizing, arranging, and usually publishing the results. 

 

Because of the high level of effort and labor that goes into genealogy, researchers at all levels have learned that it is best to share the results of their research.  A whole industry has developed around the process of sharing genealogical information.  Much of the genealogy-sharing commerce is free with much of it being on web sites such as this one.

 

The free-flow of genealogical information may cause concern among some people.  These people are usually concerned about the potential for infringement on their privacy.  While the "right to privacy" is a subject of much debate (and we are not going to engage in that debate here) suffice it to say that some people may feel strongly about the subject.  In my experience most people do not really care that a family tree might identify their existence, or their position within a family, or their date of birth, etc.  But, it would not be fair to those who may have a concern to not honor their concern.  How can we best accomplish the need to disseminate the maximum information to the majority of the researchers and still protect the concerns of those who may wish to minimize the distribution of their personal data? 

 

Our attempt to accommodate these two, sometimes divergent objectives is as follows.  Since only information that is already in the public records are used in genealogy documents such as family trees, we will normally include all such information.  If, however, someone prefers that certain of their own information not be included, we will try to honor any requests by removing the data and information in question.  In our effort to accommodate requests for limiting disclosure there may be cases where we may need to use our best judgment regarding the request.  For example, we would expect to honor requests for the removal of dates of birth or other personal information for living people.  We would probably not remove a name of an individual, however, thereby denying the existence of that person. 

 

Clearly, this area is one where we need to be aware of personal preferences.  If you find information that is incorrect in any of the family trees (or in any other part of the web site, for that matter) you are requested to call it to our attention.  Similarly, if you find information relating to you that you prefer not be included, please call it to our attention.  The most direct route would be to contact the web-master of this site.  The e-mail addresses is available by clicking on Richard Harrington.

 

Your comments on this and any other subject are always appreciated.
