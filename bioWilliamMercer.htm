Biographical Sketch of William Mercer

 

 

BioSKETCH: William Mercer

SOURCE:  HISTORY OF TYLER COUNTY, WEST VIRGINIA TO 1984, page 376-378

          William Mercer (1788-1870) was born near Winchester at Front Royal, Virginia.  While he was quite young his family moved to Washington County and Greene County, Pa.  Here William grew to manhood, apprenticed as a cooper (making casks or barrels), and learned about farming. In 1810, he married Betsy Houston (or Huston) of Greene County, and their daughter Sarah Ann was born in 1811.

          In June 1812 he enlisted at Morgantown and served 18 months (June 1812-March 1814) serving as a private in the War of 1812, in the 12th Regiment, Virginia Volunteer Infantry on duty in Canada & New England States.  Upon his return to Monongalia County in 1814, he and wife Betsy were divorced.

          In June 1815 William married Margaret Metz (born 1798 in Greene County, PA).  Soon after, he and Peggy leased 140 acres on Day's Run off Dunkard Creek west of Blacksville, cleared part of the land for crops and built a cabin.  They planted timothy, flax, wheat and garden crops, acquired some cows, sheep, horses, and began their family during the 1820's and 30's.

          Records show they were typically self-sufficient settlers making their own clothing, shoes, furniture, soap, butter and other necessities.  Any excess was traded at the general store for luxuries like coffee, spices, dishes, books, gun powder and  shot, or hunting knives.  According to local tales, Peggy became a heroine of local tales by fending off some Indians who apparently tried to harm her family one day.

          After several years of legal hassles regarding the lands they leased from the owner's heirs, and a fire which destroyed their cabin, William & Peggy packed their remaining belongings and seven children, 5 sons and 2 daughters, headed for a new beginning in Tyler County in 1839.  The site of their first home here is uncertain, but in 1850 William was granted 160 acres of Bounty Land for his military service which was excised in the area known as Wolf Pen near Middlebourne.  As their children matured, married, and started their own farms and families, they tended to remain nearby.   Some split up the original farm, and took over for the aging William.  He was 82 when he died March 30, 1870 and was buried in the Mercer Cemetery, also referred to as Wolf Pen Cemetery, which is located on a very high ridge amidst the farms below.  Several generations of Mercers and related families have since joined him there.  However, his widow

Peg may be interred over in Pleasants County where she went to live in 1875 and died at son Labon's home mid-January 1883, age 85.  Her resting site is unknown.

Provided by Betty Latta Kitchen
