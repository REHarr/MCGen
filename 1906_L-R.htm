The Sentinel, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, November 15, 1906

                             SIX LIVES SNUFFED OUT

          Cleveland, O., Nov.13Six men were killed and five seriously injured when a boiler in the power house of the Lake Shore railroad in Collinwood, a suburb of Cleveland, blew up. The men were working close to the boiler building the foundation for a dynamo, when the explosion occurred. They were all in the mouth of a subway facing, the end which blew out of the boiler, and were scalded to death by the immense volume of steam which shot out. The dead: MAX CRAWFORD, electrician; ALBERT BLOOM, carpenter; A. J. LATTA, pipe fitter; PAUL O. LANCIONI, laborer; GIOVANNNI PACIONI, laborer; JULIUS WAEDER, pipe fitter. Engineers at the plant say the explosion was due to the formation of a mud ring in the filtering apparatus which clarifies the water before its passage into the boiler.

 

Landefeld, Wilbert Ralph Landefeld

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, March 1, 1906

          Died on February 23, at Miltonsburg, Ohio, from scarlet rash, Wilbert Ralph, son of August and Barbara Landefeld, aged 3 years and 4 days.

Lauer, Theobald Lauer

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, June 28, 1906

          Mr. Theobald Lauer, aged 73 years, died at his home at Warner, Washington county June 15, 1906, after an illness of about six weeks.

          Deceased was born in Germany and came to America many years ago.

          He married Miss Elizabeth Boesshar January 6, 1855. To them were born ten childrenthree sons and seven daughters.

          His wife, three daughters and one son preceded him to the better world.

          Mr. Lauer was a kind and loving husband and father and leaves a large circle of friends. One brother Mr. Daniel Lauer, of Warner, and one sister, Mrs. Charles Holm of St. Louis, Missouri, are among the bereaved.

          The sons and daughters remaining thank the kind friends and neighbors for their willing assistance during the sickness and death of their father.

Little, Oscar F. Little

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, August 30, 1906

          Oscar F. Little, aged 69 years, died Thursday morning, August 23, 1906. He had been in feeble health for several weeks and suffered considerable pain, but the end came peacefully.

          Mr. Little was one of the oldest citizens of Woodsfield and spent most of his life here.

          He enlisted in the beginning of the late rebellion in Company C, 25th Ohio Regiment and served his country faithfully until the close of the war.

          He was a member of the Christian church for many years.

          Always kind and obliging he had made many friends.

          He was unmarried. A brother, Mr. H. B. Little of New Matamoras, and a sister, Mrs. Samuel Grimshaw of Havensville, Kansas and many other relatives and friends are left to mourn his death.

          The funeral services took place Saturday from the residence of his nephew Sheriff Charles O. Little, and were conducted by Elder J. A. Hines, assisted by Rev. W. L. Oliver.

Longwell, Sopha Longwell

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, January 4, 1906

          At Sardis on Saturday morning, December 16, 1905, occurred one of the most horrible accidents ever witnessed by the people of this county.

          Sopha Longwell, aged 16 years, while standing before an open fire, fainted, and fell into the fire. There being no one in the room at the time of the young girls misfortune she remained unconscious in the awful flames for an unknown period of time. When discovered, she was immediately removed and Dr. S. C. Griffith of this place was summoned who rendered the best possible medical aid. Her friends anxious for her recovery, though death manifested itself the only relief, rendered every possible assistance but all to no avail. The sad affair resulted in her death which occurred Tuesday, December 19, at 5 oclock a.m.

          The funeral services were conducted by Rev. Ross after which the remains were laid to rest in the Mt. Olive cemetery.

          Sopha always wore a cheerful face and kindly expression, and will be sadly missed in the home, the school, the Sabbath school and throughout the entire community.

          Her mother having preceded her to the realm of the skies about thirteen years ago she leaves to mourn the loss of her departure, her father, one brother, one sister, a number of relatives and a host of friends who realize that she was the flower of the community and is not dead, but simply removed by the hand of God.             L. F. G.

 

Lucas, Elna Odessa Lucas

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, January 31, 1907

          Elna Odessa, daughter of Mr. and  Mrs. Arthur Lucas of Saltpetre, Ohio, has been called from our midst to the world of the great beyond. Little Elna, as she was called, was burned by her clothes catching fire at the grate and did not seem bad until convulsions set in. Everything that loving hands and medical aid could do was done but of no avail. How different however are the feelings with which we view the death of the young! How unnatural, how mysterious, how cruel it seems! The very law of decay and death even appear to have been violated. Who can picture the gloom of a household from which a tender, beautiful plant full of life and sweetness and beauty has been suddenly cut down.

          The subject of this sketch was born August 24, 1904 and died December 19, 1906, being two years, three months and twenty-five days old. She leaves to mourn her death, father, mother and six brothers. But our loss is her eternal gain. We would say to the sorrowing, weep not for the kind Shepherd hath said; Blessed are they that die in the Lord. Many nights will pillows be wet with tears of anguish. Father, mother and brothers will rest uneasy upon their couch. Oh, such loving remembrance of that one who has gone from them forever!  Did I say gone forever? Just gone before to that beautiful home to await their coming where there will be no more parting, no more sorrowing, no more tears, but there will be one bright and happy day forever more.

          Services were conducted at the Masterton church by Rev. Wharton. Interment was made in the cemetery at that place where she was laid to rest to await the resurrection morn.                     H.T.B.

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated December 27, 1906

          Two sad and fatal accidents occurred near Masterson Tuesday of last week.

          A little daughter of Mr. and Mrs. Arthur Lucas was reaching for some article on the mantel when her clothing caught fire and before the flames could be extinguished she was so badly burned that death ensued in a few hours.

          The second accident was the fatal burning of a four year old daughter of Mr. and Mrs. Thomas Burton. The family was moving their household goods and while the last load was being arranged the little girl and her brother remained in the room. The child made a whirl in front of the open grate and her clothing caught fire. The brother ran to the door and gave the alarm and the mother with great presence of mind took the child in her arms and smothered the flames but not in time to save the child. Death came to its relief in a short time.

          [ Both children died December 18, 1906]

 

Ludwig, Charlotte C. Ludwig nee Webber

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, March 1, 1906

          On Thursday morning, February 22, 1906, the death angel entered our home and called our dear beloved mother to her home of eternal rest.

          Charlotte C. Ludwig, nee Webber, was the daughter of Mr. and Mrs. Philip Webber. She was born January 24, 1862, and was reared to womanhood at Lewisville.

          She was united in marriage to Louis Ludwig of Miltonsburg on November 9, 1880, who still survives her. She had been in poor health for several months, but no one thought her end was so near until a few days previous to her departure, when she was stricken ill and continued to grow worse until God released her of her sufferings and called her to her home of eternal happiness, where no sorrows or sufferings ever come. The cause of her death was tuberculosis. All that loving hands could do was done, but to no avail. She bore her sickness patiently and was never heard to utter a word of complaint.

          She leaves to mourn her sad departure a husband and six children: Mrs. Annie Ackerman of Monroefield; Minnie, George, David, William and Sophia at home, and an aged mother, one brother and two sisters and a host of relatives and friends. She was a kind and loving mother and loved by all who knew her.

She was laid to rest Saturday amid many flowers in the family lot in the German Evangelical cemetery at Lewisville.

She united with the Evangelical church at an early age and has been a faithful member ever since.

The funeral services were conducted by Rev. F. A. Meusch of Miltonsburg, taking for his text, St. Luke 1st chapter and 45th verse.

Our once happy home is now robed in mourning and we realize the sorrow of desolation that rest over us when such a dear one is called away, but we can only say Gods will be done. The loss of such a dear one shall never be forgotten by us, but our only consolation is that mamma is in heaven and if we live for our God we shall meet her in her eternal home never more to part.                               A FRIEND

Marple, Charles O. Marple

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, January 4, 1906

          Charles Oliver, son of O.H. and Alice G. Marple, died in Zanesville, Ohio, December 15, 1905, aged three years and six months.

          Little Oliver had a very sweet disposition which won for him many friends. He was very fond of music and often his sweet childish voice was heard mingling with his sisters in beautiful songs. He was too fair for earth, so the angels came one day and bore him away to live among the pure and good forever. His last words were mammas boy, papas boy. No doubt he is now enjoying the greatest happiness in that beautiful country where no sad farewells are ever spoken, and often will he watch at the gates of pearl and wonder when will papa and mamma and sisters come to stay with the little Oliver they loved so well on earth.

          The floral offerings were very beautiful, reminding us of the fadeless flowers in that fair home where darling Oliver shall live forever.

          Funeral services were conducted by Rev. A. M. Courtney of the Second street M.E. church, after which the precious body was laid to rest in the beautiful Greenwood cemetery.                   A.C.

Marty, Clara Catherine Marty nee Kraft  2nd obit

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, March 22, 1906

          Tuesday evening, March 13, 1906, the death angel came and claimed for its victim Mrs. Katie Marty, nee Kraft, wife of Mr. R. A. Marty. While death came suddenly yet it was not wholly unexpected as she has been in poor health for nearly a year with the dreaded disease consumption.

          Mrs. Marty was born near Round Bottom, Monroe county, September 2d, 1878 and died at Brookside, Ohio, aged 27 years, 6 months and 11 days.

          She was married to Mr. R. A. Marty April 4, 1900. A husband and one son four years old survive her, beside father, mother, one sister and one brother and six-half sisters and one half brother and a host of friends who mourn her death.

          When about thirteen years of age she united with the M. E. church at Chapel Hill.

          Her remains were brought down Tuesday to her fathers and the funeral services were conducted Friday by Rev. E. O. Morris of Clarington and interment was made at the Chapel Hill cemetery.

          The floral offerings were beautiful. In her death the community loses a good neighbor, a husband, a faithful wife, and child a good mother, but weep not for she is not lost, only gone before.  Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord, yea saith the spirit they do rest from their labors and their works do follow them.

Matz, Herman F. Matz  2nd obit

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, January 11, 1906

          Herman Matz, youngest son of Lewis Matz of Lewisville, Ohio, was born September 12, 1882. About November 1st of last year he became sick, and after almost two months of severe illness was thought to be on the road to recovery, when he was again obliged to take his bed, and after a few weeks of illness it became evident that he was not to recover. All that human aid and neighborly love could do was done, but in vain. At all times cheerful and of good spirits he bore the cross that was laid upon him, until it was taken from him, and he entered the eternal home of the blessed on January 2, 1906, at 2 p.m. His father, four brothers, and seven sisters mourn his departure, his mother having preceded him to the eternal rest.

          Herman Matz was an active member of the Middle Church choir, a faithful teacher in the Sunday schoolin short, a young man whose loss will be keenly felt by his many friends. He was laid to rest in the Middle Church cemetery, January 4.

          Deceased was a brother of Attorney L.E. Matz of Woodsfield.

                             EULOGY OF JOHN RUBY

John Ruby is dead! Standing in the kitchen with his wife on Thursday morning, Dec. 20, he fell backward to the floor and at once became unconscious, breathing but a few times.

While Mr. Ruby was not notified of the exact moment in which the summons was to be served, he had been expecting a call of this kind for some time past, because of the imperfect action of his heart. Great as was the heart of Mr. Ruby it was compelled to yield its physical powers to the inevitable.

He leaves to mourn his loss, a wife, two sons and three daughters, and an entire community of friends and neighbors; few men will be missed from a community as will be Mr. Ruby: when any one in the vicinity, wished counsel or arbitration, he was sought and his opinion and guidance asked; and in the language of George Elliott when he said come they stepped in faith.

Mr. Ruby had three qualifications that made him the man that he was: one was a patience that was almost inexhaustible, another was a good judgement, and thirdly an honesty that was never questioned. No man received wrong council or lost anything because of any violation on his part.

The confidence of the community in Mr. Ruby may be seen when we recount the positions that have been entrusted to his care and the manner in which he filled those positions; he filled the County Commissioners office for two terms, and is acknowledged by the people of the county as having been one of the best officers in the history of the county.

When the Bureau of Accounting of the state examined the records of the various county officials and found that he was not entitled he promptly refunded the amount found due: various township offices have been entrusted to his care, and at his death, having reached his seventy-second milestone left three vacancies to be filled: one, the presidency of the German Farmers Mutual Fire Insurance Company, which said office he filled continuously from the time of its incorporation, he having been one of its charter members; one, as president of the Hannibal system of telephony, and one as trustee of a block of coal lands entrusted to his care for sale.

No man was ever more interested in his neighbors, in his respective sphere than was Mr. Ruby. When offered three dollars an acre for his coal lands, in advance of his neighbors, in order to help the promoters to get the territory under option, he promptly answered that he did not want any more money for his coal land than his neighbors.

Thus did his honesty always appear!

The question now confronts us: who shall take his place in the community in which he lived?

In the language of the poet, he has but taken his lamp and retired to his eternal resting place, yet not alone, but shall lie down with the innumerable caravanthe patriarch, the kings, the wise, the good and the hoary seers of ages past; we remain a little longer as one stays to cover the embers that still burn.

 

Ruby, John Ruby

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, December 27, 1906

          Mr. John Ruby of Ohio township died suddenly Friday of last week and was buried at Mt. Zion cemetery Sunday. Mr. Ruby had been afflicted with heart trouble for some time.

          He was one of the leading men of his township and of Monroe county. He served the people for two terms as commissioner and made one of the best officials the county ever had. Every act was conscientious with him and his aim was always to be just in every particular.

          He leaves a wife and many friends.

          His high standing in his community was evidenced by the unusually large attendance at the last sad rites.

          [d. December 21, 1906]

Ryan, T. C. Ryan

Spirit of Democracy, Woodsfield, Ohio, dated, May 31, 1906

          News was received here last week of the death of Prof. T. C. Ryan of Columbus.

          Prof. Ryan in former years was an instructor in our schools and had many friends here who were sorely shocked to learn of his death.

The remains were taken to Senecaville for burial.
