Oscar (Heinrich Ferdinand) Frank

 

It all started with a young man named Heinrich Ferdinand Francke who emigrated from Saxony, Germany to America in the 1840s and changed his name to Oscar Frank. Photography had been invented by about 1839 but was not yet available to the general public. Realizing that he would most likely never be seen again by his family, this picture was painted as a likeness of Heinrich, aka, Oscar, as the family communicated by letters across the Atlantic Ocean. The original picture of Heinrich Ferdinand Francke, aka, Oscar Frank, now hangs in the home of a triple great grandson of the Devore family. The above is a photograph of that painting.

 

457

The Old Home Place was abandoned when visited by Oscars grandsons, Ed (left) and John Frank, in November, 1977

 

Oscar Frank, his wife, Carolina, and the family of 8 children bought the farm on 2 April 1869 that this house occupied. It is not clear whether the house already existed at the time of purchase or whether Oscar built it. When the family moved into the house, the 8

children ranged in age from 6-months to 17-years old. Only one of their 9 children, Oscar John Frank, was born in this house. The house became known as The Old Home Place and is no longer standing.

 

August Herman Frank & Louisa Young

August Herman Frank and Louisa Young were 6th cousins. They shared the same 5th great grandparents, Franz and Susanna Reeb-Brubach. Franz Brubach was born in Zurich, Switzerland. His wife, Susanna Reeb, was German. Augusts mother was Carolina Brubach and Louisas grandmother was Catharine Barbara Jung/Young. Being 6th cousins, their relationship was distant very likely, too distant for them to know that they were related. They were related as follows:

The Young and Frank families almost certainly knew each other. In addition to both families sharing the Brubach bloodline, they also lived about 1-mile apart (as the crow flies), although their houses were separated by two ridges and two valleys. Both families came from the same German village of Rumbach, they both spoke German and little or no English. They were both of protestant backgrounds and they would have both gone to the same school and church, if they went to either. Both Louisa and August were the first generation in their respective families to be born in America.

Whether through family relationship or one or more of the above community relationships, August and Louisa became friends; good friends; good enough friends that they conceived a child that Louisa named William Willie Edward Young, who was born on 20 May 1881. Louisa and August never married for 4-years following the conception. When each married, they married their own chosen spouses in 1884. In the years immediately following Williams conception and birth, Louisa and August waged a bitter legal battle in which Louisa won a verdict that declared August to be

Williams father and $300 settlement for child support.

 

458

 

August maintained throughout his life that he was not Williams father, a claim that was proven incorrect when the author, Richard E. Harrington, organized a DNA test between two male members, one from each family line, that proved at a high level of confidence, that August H. Frank was the father of William E. Young. More details of this saga can be found on page-72 of this book.

The legal, cultural and moral aspects of Louisa and Augusts liaison were settled about 135-years ago. But, the impact on history and population continues to increase. As this book goes to press, the descendants of that August and Louisa union, number about 132 people who share both of their DNA.
