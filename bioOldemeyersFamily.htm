Biographical Sketch of the Oldemeyers Family

 

How the Oldemeyers Came to America from The Netherlands

 

          A group of immigrants from The Netherlands sailed on the English ship, The Southerner [1]  from Rotterdam on September 24, and arrived in New York City, NY, November 17, 1846. The ship weighed 276 tons and Tully Crosby was the captain. The ship was a 3 masted sailing ship. The Immigrants were under the leadership of the 35 year old Reverend A. C. Van Raalte, a Christian Reformed Minister.

          The Oldemeyer family came from Overijsel Province in The Netherlands. They were Hendrik Oldemeyer, listed as a farmer on the passenger list, [2] his wife, Dina Schepers Oldemeyer, and their son, Jan. Hendrik and Dina were 40 years old and Jan was 7 at the time they sailed for America.

          The immigrants, after arriving in New York on November 17, 1846, left by steamer for Albany, NY; thence via Buffalo and Cleveland to Detroit. Rev. Van Raalte went to the future site of Holland, Michigan in December, 1846. In January 1847 he returned to Detroit to collect his followers. During his absence he had procured work for the men at St.Clair, where a steamboat was being built. In the same month all went to Allegan, via Kalamazoo. The women and children were left in Allegan and the men proceeded to Black Lake arriving about February 12. The women and children joined them after shelters were built. In March, 1847, two more parties of immigrants joined them.

          An autobiographical sketch describing the voyage is preserved in the Netherland Museum in Holland, Museum in Holland, Michigan. It was written by Henry Cook (Hendrick Kok), whose parents were also passengers. [3]

          Dina Schepers Oldemeyer passed away shortly after the spring of 1847 in Holland, Michigan [4] and later Hendrik married Hendrina Schutmaat, who came to Holland with an uncle very soon after the Van Raalte group. Hendrik and Hendrina were married in Holland, Michigan by the Rev.Van Raalte. They had ten children together. The children were born in Holland, Michigan except as noted below. Their names were:

 

Gerrit Dina, born in 1848

Garret Jan, born Sept. 10, 1851

Hendrick Jan (Henry John), born Aug. 29, 1853 in Soveriesel, MI [5]

Susan, born Aug. 31, 1856

Hendrick (Henry), born Apr. 4, 1857

Benjamin, born Mar. 9, 1862 in Grand Rapids, MI [5]

Hein, born Mar. 9, 1862 (twin to Benjamin)

Jan (John), born 1866

Herman, born Apr. 24, 1867

Hannah “Anna”, born 1868 [6]

 

          The family moved to Holland, Nebraska probably in late 1873 and Hendrik bought 160 acres from the Burlington & Missouri River Railroad of Nebraska at $9 per acre on 1/26/1874. [7] The family had cleared 160 acres of timber in Michigan. Hendrik had bought it for $0.50 per acre on 3/10/1857. [7] Hendrik passed away not long after. It is supposed that they went to Nebraska for easier plowing. The sons all had farms in Lancaster County within a short distance of each other. In 1880, Henry J. bought 80 acres from the Burlington & Missouri River Railroad of Nebraska also at $9 per acre. [7]

          There is no tombstone on either Hendrik’s or Hendrina’s grave. They were buried in the Holland, Nebraska, Cemetery, and the graves were originally marked with wooden markers and a picket fence around the family plot, which have long since disappeared. Hendrik and Dina’s son, Jan, is buried in the same plot. There seems to be no cemetery record or way of locating their graves. [8]

          In later years, Hendrina was a little crippled lady and lived with her son, Herman, and his family until her death at the age of 77 in February of 1904 and was buried in Holland Cemetery Feb. 10, 1904. [9] Also buried in the Holland Cemetery was Henry John Oldemeyer and his wife, Neltje, Henry Oldemeyer, son of Garret Jan and Anna, and his wife, Anna, Anna Liesveld, Gertrude Oldemeyer and Sarah Oldemeyer, daughters of Henry John and Neltje, Hannah’s only child, Cora Newman as well as a stone for Hannah with a birth year on it, but no death year, (is she buried there?) and a couple of other relatives. [10]

          The Oldemeyer name used to be written Oldemeijer. In Dutch there is no y, but j has the same sound.

          All the children listed above were married in Lancaster County except for Hannah.

          This information was taken from a letter written by Mick Vander Griend’s mother, Hendricka (Reka) Oldemeyer, daughter of Herman Oldemeyer and Nellie Klerk and typed up by Joyce Koenig Chapman. It was updated in March, 2005 by Don Hansen and the notes below are from his records.
