Beabout, John Beabout (Obit 1)

Source:  Spirit of Democracy  February 24, 1858

Horrible!  --  On the morning of the 10th, three miles north east of Beallsville in this county, a man was found in the road frozen, he was lying on his face with his hands up to the wrists, and the greater part of his head and face in the mud; in which condition he was found dead and frozen so tight that he had to be chopped out with a mattock.  At the time our informant left, the body had not been recognized, the clothing were so completely covered with mud that it was impossible to distinguish their color.  Cause is not yet known.

     Since writing the above, we have been informed that his name was JOHN BEABOUT, and leaves a wife and three small children.    ------Herald.

Booth, Asahel Booth

Source:  Spirit of Democracy  March 24, 1858

Death of ASAHEL BOOTH --  At his residence in Salem township, in this county, on Sunday night last, ASAHEL BOOTH, aged 79 years, 11 months and 4 days.

     The circumstances attending the death of this aged and respected citizen are truly melancholy.  On Thursday, the 11th inst., he went out on his lands where he had a man employed in felling timber, and while looking on, as he supposed, from a safe position, a tree that was being chopped down, broke across the kerf [sic] and fell in a different direction from what was expected.  The deceased, thinking he could not escape the falling tree where he stood threw himself under an old log, the end of which was a few feet from the ground.  Unfortunately the log was too much decayed to sustain the falling tree, and broke so as to let much of the weight of the tree and log come upon the body of the deceased.  He lingered in partial unconsciousness until 12 o’clock of Sunday night, when death released him from his sufferings.

     The deceased was born near Hartford, in the State of Connecticut, and emigrated to the city of Wheeling 51 years ago, where he was for 25 years, engaged in the mercantile business.  He then removed to Clarington, in this county, where he engaged in similar pursuits.  And it can be said of him, with great truth, that in all his intercourse with his fellow men he acted as a Christian and an honest man.  No man ever had more of the confidence and respect of the community in which he lived than the deceased, and it would afford the writer a melancholy satisfaction to be able to speak of him as his merits and many virtues demand.  But his worth as a citizen and a neighbor may be told in few words:  He was a christian in whom there was no guile, and the noblest work of God --  an honest man.  And although, in the course of nature, he could not have remained with us long, yet it is a matter of great sorrow and affliction that he was called so suddenly to his long home; but we rejoice that we can say that he has gone to his grave full of years and full of honor --  leaving behind him a name that friends and neighbors will delight to honor.

     His aged companion, to whom he was united over 55 years ago, still survives him, together with many children, grand children and great grand children, who will rise up and call him blessed.     J. R. M.

     At a meeting of the members of the Bar of Woodsfield, at the office of Hollister, Okey & Hollister, on the 12th day of March, 1858, called on account of the death of John Sinclair, Esq., on motion Edward Archbold, Esq., was called to the Chair, and Simson Hollister was appointed Secretary.

     On motion a committee was appointed consisting of William Okey, William F. Hunter and Nathan Hollister, Esqs., to report resolutions expressive of the feelings of this meeting on account of the decease of a brother member of the Bar.  Whereupon the committee reported the following preamble and resolutions, which were unanimously adopted:

     Whereas, it has pleased Almighty God, is the wise dispensation of His providence, to remove from amongst us JOHN SINCLAIR, Esq., one of the members of the Woodsfield Bar, in the prime of life, surrounded by a family and friends, and everything calculated to make life desirable; and whereas, in the death of Mr. Sinclair, his family has lost a kind and affectionate husband and father, in Bar an advocate of ability and courtesy, and society a philanthropist and true friend;  Therefore,

     Resolved, That the members of this Bar deeply sympathise [sic] with the family of the deceased in this their sad bereavement.

Died -- In Cincinnati, O., on Thursday morning the 30th of September 1858, Mr. SAMUEL STEPHEN, aged about 64 years.

     The deceased was born in Belmont county, in this State, on the first day of December 1795, and was removed with his parents to Seneca township in this county, at an early age, and on his shoulders rested, in an eminent degree the task of opening up a home in the wilderness, and most nobly did his strong arm prove adequate to the task, brooking every difficulty and suffering all the privitations [sic] incident to the first settlers of a new country.  In 1819 he married the inestimable lady he now leaves behind him, and commenced opening up, in the forest, another home for himself and family where he has ever since resided.  From his house, the hungry was never turned empty away, nor the needy without having their wants supplied.  Mr. S. was a worthy and acceptable member of the Christian Church, and in an eminent degree did he show forth the character of the true christian, not only by precept but by example in an upright walk and a chaste conversation.  In his death the Church has lost a bright ornament and a long tried pillar, the community has lost one of its oldest and best citizens, the family has lost a faithful and confiding husband, and a loving, kind and indulgent father.  For the last few months of his life he suffered much from disease contracted many years since by fatigue, exposure and excessive labor, and a short time before his death he went to Cincinnati in company with his lady for medical treatment.  His remains were brought home by his lady and intered [sic] in the family burial ground.   J.M.
